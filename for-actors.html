Workshop Your Cast.
Elevate Your Project.

Pushpendra works both on set and in pre-production, bringing actors to a place of deep character readiness before the camera rolls. He has workshopped ensemble casts for Netflix's Class and Kohrra Season 1, for Anup Singh's Qissa and Song of the Scorpions, with Golshifteh Farahani and Irrfan Khan among the cast, for Rohena Gera's Sir and the upcoming Stay, for Abhijit Mazumdar's Body, for which Manoj Sharma won Best Actor, and for the upcoming Don't Be Shy, directed by Sreeti Mukerji and produced by Alia Bhatt for Amazon Prime. For Individual Actors

Trained by the World's Best.
Now Available to You.

Pushpendra trained as an actor at FTII — India's premier film school — and under Barry John, one of India's most influential theatre practitioners. Over the years he has trained in workshops with some of the world's leading practitioners: at Berlinale Talents — as the first Indian actor ever invited to the programme — with Jean-Louis Rodrigue, the movement coach who worked with Leonardo DiCaprio on J. Edgar; with Jasmila Žbanić, Golden Bear winner at Berlin and Oscar-nominated for Quo Vadis, Aida?, on acting in close-up; and with Alby James OBE on script analysis.

Decades of working as an actor, filmmaker and teacher have refined this into a process that consistently works — not borrowed from any single school, but tested across film sets, rehearsal rooms and training sessions worldwide.

That process — applied entirely to one actor and one performance — is available as private, confidential coaching. Whether you are beginning your journey or preparing for a specific role, the work is built entirely around where you are and what you need.

The Seven Pillars of Our Training

A State of Flow, Earned Through Practice

Core Mechanics

Somatic Anchoring

Grounding and centering work begins the process, followed by breath and somatic awareness. Actors learn to scan their own bodies, head to toe, noticing sensation without judgment — building the ability to consciously command focus into the body rather than letting it react separately from the mind. The goal is a body that adapts on cue to what a scene demands, not one that betrays the actor with its own unconscious habits.

Core Mechanics

Internal Monologue

Every character wears a mask — the version of themselves they choose to show. Using mask work and improvisation, actors learn to play the contradiction between what a character performs outwardly and what their internal monologue is actually saying underneath. A character can be calm on the surface while their inner voice is in crisis — and it is that exact friction, held and played simultaneously, that the camera reads as truth.

Core Mechanics

Vocal Resonance

A close-up reveals the hidden lies, and it is first reflected in the projected voice. Modern, high-definition microphones can catch the slightest of whispers, removing any need to project or push the voice. Through diaphragmatic breathing and an awareness of the body's natural resonators, actors learn to drop forced theatrical projection and find a grounded, intimate vocal quality that conveys absolute psychological truth on screen.

Core Mechanics

Applied Agility & Behaviour

Film is a director's medium, and an actor who can only work one way will struggle. Through advanced improvisation, status work and animal exercises, actors build the range to adapt to different directorial styles and characterisations — learning to move like a chameleon between worlds, rather than performing the same instrument in every room.

Core Mechanics

Approaching a Screenplay

Before an actor says a single line, there is work to be done. This module trains actors to decode a script in full — genre, theme, the world the story inhabits, a character's background, their wants, and the obstacles and urgency standing in their way. At the centre of it all sits relationship: between characters, and between a character and the space they occupy. Within this larger discipline lies a lesson Pushpendra learned firsthand from Irrfan Khan on the set of Qissa — that a character's internal need and their external want are rarely the same thing, and finding that gap is where a performance stops being acted and starts being lived.

Core Mechanics

Sculpting the Frame

Actors learn to think like painters, photographers and sculptors, understanding how a figure occupies space, holds weight, and creates dynamic tension within a frame. Moving beyond simply hitting a mark, this module teaches you to compose your own presence on screen.

Core Mechanics

Business of Acting & Casting

Actors learn the craft of blocking and framing, and adapt their performance according to the camera lens — understanding what the camera can and cannot see. The module closes with the business of acting itself: how casting actually works, and what it takes to navigate that world as a working professional.

For Kids & Parents

Where Young Performers
Find Their Voice

Acting training for children is not about making them perform, it is about helping them discover themselves.

Drawing from his years with Imago Theatre, Imago Theatre-in-Education — Barry John's company — and Yellow Cat Theatre Company, founded by Sukhesh Arora, Pushpendra brings a uniquely sensitive, inclusive and joyful approach to training young performers. His work has included children and participants who were blind, deaf, non-verbal, or living with physical and neuromotor disabilities.

The training is built specifically for screen — films, OTT and other image media — alongside organic, game-based exploration. Safe, nurturing and deeply professional.

Pushpendra takes on children's coaching selectively — in collaboration with filmmakers and production houses working with young performers, and with theatre groups and educators looking to introduce children to screen.
